The 10× Day

What happened when our team turned off their IDEs for a day — and what I'm not yet concluding from it.

For the past few months my team has been moving our custom tools from one application base to another. It's the kind of work that sounds straightforward but isn't — each tool has its own quirks, its own state, its own little knot of business logic that nobody fully remembers writing. We had settled into a steady cadence: roughly one developer-week per application. Predictable enough that I could plan against it.

Last week we ran an Innovation Day with a single rule: turn off your IDE. Let the AI do the work.

I'd expected the day to feel like a stunt. We'd compare notes, talk about what worked, maybe ship a half-finished proof of concept. That's not what happened.

By the end of the day, each developer had moved two applications.

10×
Five developer-days per application became half a developer-day. A 10× change in throughput for this specific kind of work, on this specific day.

Which is exactly the moment I want to be careful.

What I'm not concluding

A single Innovation Day is a single data point. Before I extrapolate, here are the questions I'm holding open:

1. The code is moved, not yet running in production. Migration complete is not the same as running clean in prod. Until those applications ship and run without regression, the 10× is provisional. The win we measured was the front-loaded work — the typing, the wiring, the boilerplate. If we discover a wave of follow-up fixes in QA, the effective ratio comes down.

2. Migration is the easy case for AI. Moving an existing application from one base to another is a translation problem with a clear source and a clear target. That's the kind of task large language models are best at. It does not follow that greenfield design work, ambiguous requirements gathering, or production incident response will see anything like 10×. I'd be surprised if they did.

3. We picked a single category of work. The applications we moved were comparable enough that the result is internally consistent, but I haven't yet run the same experiment on harder cases — apps with the gnarliest business logic, the most fragile state, the most legacy quirks. Those might still be one-dev-week problems.

4. One day is one day. Sustained productivity is a different claim than peak productivity. The thing I actually want to know — and don't yet — is whether the team can hold any meaningful fraction of this gain across normal weeks, with the usual interruptions, meetings, and ambient complexity. Innovation Day is a controlled environment. The rest of the calendar is not.

5. The constraint mattered more than the AI. "IDEs off" wasn't theater. It forced the team out of muscle memory and into a different working pattern — narrating intent in natural language, reviewing AI output as the primary craft, batching work differently. I suspect the result is as much about the constraint as about the tooling. If we just say "use AI more" without changing how work flows, I'd expect a much smaller effect.

What I am measuring next

The post-Innovation-Day plan is straightforward. Over the next 6–8 weeks, on the same migration project, I want to see:

  • How much of the 10× peak shows up in regular weeks. Even a sustained 3× would be a significant business result. I'd rather measure the floor honestly than claim the ceiling.
  • What the production-readiness cost looks like. Time-to-merge is a proxy. Time-to-clean-running-prod is the real number.
  • Where the approach breaks. Which of the harder applications eat the gain. That's where the next round of process design has to happen.

I'll report the answers, including the ones that aren't flattering.

The takeaway

When you remove a tool people are used to, you sometimes discover what the tool was actually costing. That's the part of the day I keep thinking about. We didn't get faster because the AI is smarter than our developers — our developers are excellent, and the AI on its own would have produced nothing. We got faster because the team was forced into a different working pattern, and that pattern turned out to be ~10× as efficient for one specific kind of work.

The right conclusion isn't "AI changes everything." It's narrower and more useful: for translation-shaped problems, with the right working pattern, the ceiling is much higher than I had been planning for.

That's worth measuring carefully — and worth designing around, even before the picture is complete.
