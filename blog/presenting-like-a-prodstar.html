Presenting Like A ProdStar

I can still remember early in my a career going to a department head meeting and giving presentation on what was happening with my product. To set the stage, I am actually one of those people who likes to speak in public so I was not nervious about speaking and I had a fairly good understanding of what my product did and the direction we were heading. However, after the meeting, my boss pulled me aside a told me many of the department heads were confused from my presentation and had more questions then answers about the direction of my product and worse were concerned I was not the right person to managing the product.

This completely devestated me, I kept thinking back on what I had said, double-checked my slides to see what I had written and racked my brain replaying the incident over and over again trying to find out what I had done wrong. As I was going through this, one of my coworkers saw me struggling to make sense of what was going on and came over to help. I still remember the advice they gave me of telling me to forget about the presentation in the past and focus on the next one I was going to have to give in a couple of weeks. During this time my friend helped me work on my presentation and practice a few things to help me get ready for the next presentation. Some of the things we went over where:

Select the right presentation method

For many of us, when we hear we are going to be giving a presentation, the first place we start at is with PowerPoint, but is this really the best way to get your message across to the people you are presenting too? In the case above, the meeting was amoung several different departments and everyone submitted all of their slide decks to the person running the meeting and they just combined them altogether.

However, as I have gone through my career, I have tried to find different ways to help get my message across to the people I was presenting to. Recently, while other people we going through their slides about their products, I opted to give out a one page handout about what was going on with our product. On it was a map of everything we had identified we needed to work on and then our progress of where we were on the map. Later in the day I stopped by one of the people's office and on their board they had pinned up my map and said they were looking forward to my next update to be able to see the progress.

Know the audience you are presenting to

In my first example, where I failed miserably, I had not taken the time to know the audience or the departments I was going to be presenting to. This happened to be one of the first times I had ever spoken with people from the Sales and Marketing departments. I was use to speaking to a much more technical crowd and had add to much of this information into my presentation without speaking much of how what were going to build was going to be sold or marketed to our end users. Once I realized this mistake, I went and spend some time with the different departments to make sure I knew what it was they were looking for in my presentation.

This is something I still do every time I am going to give a presentation. I will read through the list of the people who are going to be in the meeting and see who I know is going to be there and if there are people I do not recognize I will do my homework to see what it is about my prodcut they are going to be the most interested in. This helps me make sure that even if I do not have a specfic slide about their concern, I will make sure it is in my notes to talk about.

Recently I needed to give a presentation to several of the C suite executive in my company on one of the backend projects my team had been working developing. After coming up with the initial presentation, I took it to my developers to have them review the items I was presenting to make sure what I was saying was accurate. Much of the feedback I received was that I had not gone into enough technical detail on many of the parts of the project. I had to agree with them that I had not gone into much technical detail at all because I was looking at who my audiance was. Most of the people in meeting did not have a technical background and were really only needing a quick summation of where we were with our product. Once my developers remembered who the audiance was they agreed the technical detail did not need to be added.

Talk more than is on your slide and do it confidently

All too often you will go to a presentation where you will see the person just reading their slides, almost word for word. Then when they are through with reading everything on the page, they will flip to the next slide and begin reading again. Occasionally they might make a comment that is not written on their slide but these are often far a few between.

I like to go with the rule of reading very little off my slides and talk and make comments more about the subject of my slides. This is where, if I do not have a slide specificly designed for the groups in the room, I will make note to bring up how this latest feature is going to help sales and markting or what improvements our customer service representives are going to see from our next release. This is where you can make your presentaion come alive and you can get the people you are presenting to involved and excited about your topic (You might even be able to throw in a couple of those technical details you fought so hard to keep out if you see the audiance is engaged in what you are talking about).

Dovetailing into this is to make sure you speak confidently about your topic. Your company is looking to you to be the voice of this product and bring it to life for everyone. As you speak with confidence those people in the room will feel that in your presentation and in many times will remember that way more than anything you might have put up on any slides or talked about through your presentation.

As a Product Manager you are never going to get out of giving presentation about your product and where you are going, but hopefully with some of this advice it can help make it so you do not go through same experience I had when I was first starting out.
