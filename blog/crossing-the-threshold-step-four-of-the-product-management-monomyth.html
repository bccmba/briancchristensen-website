Crossing The Threshold - Step Four of the Product Management Monomyth

In Lewis Carroll's novel Alice's Adventures in Wonderland, we get to follow the adventure of Alice as chases after the white rabbit. We get to meet such amazing characters as the Mad Hatter, the Cheshire Cat, and the very grumpy Queen of Hearts. However, this whole adventure begins with a very big step. Alice is following the white rabbit when he ducks down into a hole. Most people would have given up, but not Alice. She follows the rabbit into the hole and crosses the threshold from her world into the magical world of Wonderland.

It is the crossing of the threshold I would like to go over today. Much like Alice, as a Product Manager, there is going to be a time where you are going to need to cross your own threshold. Now you may not end up wondering through anywhere near as magical as Wonderland, but the adventure you take your product on is going to be just as magical. Although, unlike Alice, as a Product Manager we need to make sure we do not dive right into the rabbit hole we need to plan our adventure a little better.

Feature Discovery

After the Product Manager has received their Call to Action and with the help of their Supernatural Aid, the first step for the team is to do discovery work on the new feature. For everyone involved in the project, this is going to mean different tasks.

For the Product Manager, this going to mainly be gathering requirements and negotiations with those involved. Whether the stakeholder presenting the new feature is external to the team or someone from the team, it is important to identify the "what" and the "why" you are going to be building the feature. It is also important to identify a good Minimal Viable Product or Prototype (MVP) everyone can agree on. This MVP should be something that when in production, will give the stakeholders the ability to see the "what" and "why" in action. It may not be the final solution, but it is still giving them some lift on what they are looking for.

For the development team, this is where they get to use their magic. Armed with the "what" and the "why" or at least a good idea of what those are, the team gets to start figuring out "how" to make the feature happen. This has always been one of my favorite things to watch as a Product Manager. Many times the stakeholders, or even myself, will come in with an idea of how we would like to see the product changed. Then I sit back and watch the team come up with their version of how they can get the work done. Most of the time, these solutions are far more elegant than anything anyone else outside of the dev team has come up with.

Build With the End in Mind

When I first started as a Product Manager, I thought the best way to work with the team was by giving them little bits of the work at a time. I did not want to stress them out with all of the complexities of the project. However, more often than not, this was just a recipient for disaster. Most of the time resulting in the development team getting frustrated with me because had they known everything upfront, they would have designed the process differently. This would then either lead to a poor user experience with the product or recoding work we had already done. Neither one of these are great situations to be in.

Now, as I am working with the team, I present everything to them at the very beginning. This way when they are working on their discovery of "how" to build the feature, they know what the outcome is supposed to be. Then there is no rework and our users can have the best experience with our product.

One other tip. Again as I was starting as a Product Manager, I was very focused and when asked to build a new feature for client A, we built the new feature for client A. However, what usually happened was this new feature did so well the company wanted to roll it out to all of our clients. The problem was we had only made it for client A and not to scale across the company. After having this happen enough times, I now always make sure even if we are only building a feature for a specific user, we make sure it will scale across all of our clients and we do not need to do any additional work.

Enjoy the Ride

Once you have taken your time to get prepared for your adventure, everyone has done their discovery work and you all know what the end is supposed to look like, the real fun is about to happen. Much like Alice, there are going to be some ups and downs, which we will discuss in the next installment. However, you are off on a new adventure with your product, be excited and get ready for a great ride.
