"The Terror" Of Product Management

Applying Product Management Principles to AMC’s The Terror: The Cost of Waiting Too Long to Make the Right Decisions

In product management, the ability to pivot quickly in the face of shifting data, user feedback, or market conditions is essential. Delayed decision-making can result in missed opportunities, reduced user satisfaction, or failure. A fascinating case study in delayed action comes from an unlikely source: AMC's television series The Terror. Based on the true story of the doomed 1845 Franklin Expedition to the Arctic, the series provides a dramatic, almost textbook, example of the dangers of indecision.

The Franklin expedition’s leadership, faced with treacherous conditions, made several critical decisions too late, sealing the fate of the entire crew. Let’s dive into how key moments in The Terror align with product management principles—and how timely decisions could have led to better outcomes.

1. Failing to Pivot Based on Data

One of the central themes in The Terror is the crew’s initial confidence in their well-equipped ships and their dismissal of warning signs as they encounter harsh weather conditions and impenetrable ice. Early on, Captain Franklin chooses to stay the course despite mounting evidence that they were becoming trapped by the ice.

In product management, this is akin to ignoring early user feedback or market trends that suggest a product is heading in the wrong direction. Instead of acknowledging these signals and adjusting the product roadmap accordingly, sticking to the original plan can lead to failure.

The lesson: Regularly assess market data, feedback, and user behavior. If the evidence suggests you’re going down the wrong path, don’t wait for disaster before pivoting. An agile approach could have saved the Franklin crew—or a product in danger of irrelevance.

2. Ignoring Customer (Crew) Concerns

In The Terror, the crew begins to express doubts about their survival as the ice tightens its grip. However, the leadership ignores these concerns, opting instead to proceed with their original, inflexible plan. This disconnect between leadership and the "customers" (the crew, in this case) is eerily familiar to the product management world, where ignoring customer feedback often leads to failure.

This lack of communication is a reminder to product managers of the importance of maintaining a tight feedback loop with users. If the product team ignores pain points, frustrations, or feature requests, users will eventually leave for better alternatives. The Terror crew voiced concerns about their resources and survival, just as users express dissatisfaction through churn or poor reviews.

The lesson: Listening to customers is critical. It may not be comfortable to acknowledge their concerns, but acting swiftly on feedback could mean the difference between success and failure.

3. Overconfidence in Existing Resources

Captain Franklin’s overconfidence in the ships and provisions mirrors a product manager’s overreliance on the success of past strategies or current resources. Leaders of the expedition believed that the state-of-the-art ships and supplies would guarantee survival, even in uncharted and hazardous territories. This reliance on "what has always worked" often blinds teams to the need for innovation and proactive risk mitigation.

In the world of product development, assuming that current technology or a static feature set will sustain the product indefinitely can be a fatal mistake. The competitive landscape is always changing, and like the Arctic ice closing in around the ships, the market can shift suddenly, freezing out those who don’t innovate.

The lesson: Assess your product’s weaknesses and gaps regularly. What worked before may not continue to work in the future. Flexibility and readiness to adapt are vital.

4. Delayed Decision-Making in the Face of Crisis

As the expedition slowly succumbs to worsening conditions, there’s a poignant moment when the leadership finally realizes that the situation is dire—but by then, it’s too late. They’ve lost valuable time that could have been used to abandon ship, seek alternatives, or even reroute the entire expedition.

This scenario directly mirrors the consequences of delayed decision-making in product management. Leaders often wait for the perfect amount of data or resist making tough calls, but in doing so, they lose valuable time in competitive markets. Waiting too long to adjust a strategy can result in missed opportunities, market share loss, or product failure.

The lesson: Don’t wait for disaster before making necessary decisions. When faced with clear risks, act quickly to pivot, adjust, or even scrap a product before it’s too late. A delayed decision can sink an entire project.

5. The Cost of Poor Leadership Vision

At the core of the expedition’s failure was poor leadership vision. Captain Franklin's failure to adapt and his insistence on following an outdated plan is a classic example of poor product leadership. In product management, the leader must have a vision but remain flexible and open to change. A rigid attachment to a product’s initial concept or an unwillingness to explore new ideas can doom the product to irrelevance.

In contrast, great product leaders understand when to pivot, innovate, and guide their teams through uncertainty. Like the expedition, if product teams can’t navigate unforeseen challenges and adapt quickly, the product may never reach its destination.

The lesson: Leadership matters. The success of a product depends on a clear vision that is also adaptive to market conditions and user feedback. Without this, even the best plans can fail.

Conclusion: Learning from The Terror

AMC’s The Terror is a chilling dramatization of the consequences of waiting too long to make the right decisions, but its lessons are incredibly relevant to product managers today. The key takeaway for any product team is that no plan is foolproof, and the ability to adapt quickly in the face of challenges is paramount.

Just as the Franklin Expedition’s delayed reactions led to their downfall, product teams must learn to be agile, prioritize customer feedback, and act decisively in the face of uncertainty. In product management, waiting too long to act can mean losing the race to competitors or delivering a product no one wants.

The ice is always closing in—how will you respond?
