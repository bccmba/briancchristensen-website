Trials - Step Five of the Product Management Monomyth

When I think of someone who had to go through trials, I usually think of Hercules. There are several different versions of Hercules and the trials he went through. However, most of them revolve around the story of him wanting to return to live with his father, Zeus, on Olympus. To do so, he had to face a pass a certain number of trials and prove his worth to the gods. Although these trials were hard to overcome and seemed impossible, in the end, he passed all the trials he was asked to do.

Product Managers, much like Hercules, will all go through trials as they work on the new features for their product. Once the team has Crossed the Threshold and begun working on their new feature the trials are sure to appear. For most teams these can be broken down into internal and external trials they have to face.

Internal Trials

Internal trials are those that happen with the team. The most common example of these kinds of trials happens when the team being to work on the new feature and starts to find out more problems with the code. Even though the team has done its discovery work upfront, most of this is superficial looking into the code. Once they begin to dig in and actually start the process of doing the work, it seems something they had not thought about or overlooked will appear. This is not to say the team did a bad job at their discovery work, it is just the nature of software development. Many times you do not know everything the code touches until you get in and start doing the work. Most of the time, this may cause minor delays, but depending on the issue it could set the team back further than initially expected.

Another internal challenge, though not a normal occurrence, is the possibility of having personal changes on the team. There are times when you may lose one of your supernatural aids in the middle of development. Any disruption from the team will cause some issues and the team has to make adjustments. This is regardless of what those changes might be, the loss, addition or swapping out of team members, all will cause some disruptions. Again, this is not a normal occurrence for most teams, however, it can happen and is something to keep in mind.

External Trials

This is where, as a Product Manager, I see the most trials come to the team as they are working on the new feature. These can be broken down into two different categories:

Changing Requirements

This is one of the most frustrating issues as a team is working on a new feature. Once the team has received the "what" and the "why" from the stakeholder, they want nothing more than to start working on the "how" and getting it completed. However, when the team is in the middle of developing the "how," if the stakeholder changes their mind about the "what" and the "why" and it no longer matches the work the team is doing this causes a ton of frustrations. A boss I worked with once described it as trying to build the airplane while it is in the air. This never works out well for anyone and causes many frustrations for both groups as the requirements change.

Within Agile there is a method and way to make changes where both groups can be reasonably satisfied. At the end of every sprint, the team has the opportunity to demo what they have worked on and the direction they are headed next. It is during this meeting where the stakeholder can review and see if we need to make any course corrections. Returning to our airplane analogy, this is where the plane is in the hanger and the team is saying where they just flew in from and where they are going to be headed next. If the stakeholder notices the plane is going in the wrong direction, they can have a conversation with the team and let them know what course corrections they need to take.

In my time working with software development teams I have never seen this meeting work out poorly. Even if the stakeholder tells the team they are headed in the wrong direction and what they have built needs to be fixed, the team is getting the immediate feedback they need to help get the product on the right path. The main difference is the two teams are working together to make the product better.

Conflicting/Change in Priorities

The other external trial the team may face is when either there is a conflicting priority to what they are currently working on or there is a change in their priorities. This is usually seen in organizations where the leadership does not understand how the software development team does work. For them, it is easy to quickly make the change this week that the company now has a new number one priority and if we need to change that again next week we can just make that change and head in this direction.

This can best be fixed by helping educate the executive committee on how software development in your organization works. You can take it further by helping them work out a schedule where no new initiatives or priority changes can take place within a certain time period. In my experience, doing this on a quarterly basis helps not only the development teams have time to actually work on and release products, but it gives the executives time to fully flesh out their next ideas. Thus making it easier to hit the ground running for the next quarter.

Trials are always going to be part of your Product Management journey, however, there are things you can do to help make these trials not impact your team as much as they possibly could. Just lIke Hercules you too will overcome your trials and will end up with a great product.
