The Wisdom of Jurassic Park: A Lesson for Product Managers

As we navigate the dynamic landscape of innovation and development, it's crucial to pause and reflect on the profound wisdom embedded in unexpected sources. Take for example a quote from a classic film that encapsulates a fundamental principle we must always keep in mind: "Your scientists were so preoccupied with whether they could that they didn't stop to think if they should."

These words, spoken by Dr. Ian Malcolm in Michael Crichton's masterpiece "Jurassic Park," resonate deeply within our realm of product management. They serve as a poignant reminder of the importance of feature considerations, responsible decision-making, and the potential consequences of our actions.

In the context of product development, it's easy to get swept away by the allure of technological advancement and the thrill of pushing boundaries. We often find ourselves consumed by questions of feasibility and capability: Can we create this feature? Can we push this technology to its limits? Can we disrupt the market with our innovation?

Yet, in our pursuit of these lofty goals, it's all too easy to overlook the more critical question: Should we? This is where the true essence of product management lies – not merely in what we can do, but in what we should do for the betterment of our users and stakeholders.

Consider the implications of rushing a product to market without fully assessing its ethical implications or potential harms. Just because we can develop a particular feature or deploy a certain technology doesn't mean that we should do so without careful consideration of its impact.

Take, for instance, the recent controversies surrounding data privacy breaches, algorithmic biases, and the unintended consequences of AI-driven decision-making. These are stark reminders of what happens when we prioritize technological capabilities over ethical considerations. In our quest for innovation, we must never lose sight of the human aspect of our work – the real people whose lives are affected by the products we create.

So, what can we, as product managers, learn from Dr. Malcolm's cautionary words? Firstly, we must cultivate a culture of feature awareness within our teams, where feature considerations are integrated into every stage of the product development lifecycle. This means fostering open discussions about the potential impacts of our decisions and actively seeking out diverse perspectives to inform our choices.

Secondly, we must embrace a mindset of responsible innovation, where we strive not only to push the boundaries of what's possible but also to ensure that our innovations align with the values of our stakeholders and users. This may require us to make difficult decisions, to resist the temptation of short-term gains in favor of long-term sustainability.

In conclusion, let us heed the wisdom of Jurassic Park and remember that as product managers, our ultimate responsibility is not just to create products, but to do so thoughtfully, ethically, and with a keen awareness of the impact our decisions may have on the world around us. By embracing this ethos, we can ensure that our innovations truly make the world a better place for all.

Here's to thoughtful innovation and guided leadership in product management.
