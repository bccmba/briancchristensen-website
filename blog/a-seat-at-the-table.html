A Seat At The Table

An idea you often hear discussed in the business world is the concept of having a seat at the table. In most instances this refers to a person or group of people who would like to have equal footing in a department as other people or groups do. Boiled down simple everyone wants to have and be respected by others within their organization.

But how can you earn a seat at this table? What are some things you can do to help ensure you have a place and are respected by others at the table? Let me offer three suggestions for you.

Spend More Time Listening And Less Time Talking

All too often I feel like people try too hard to be heard. Even if that means just agreeing with everything something someone else has said just so you can be heard. When the opportunity presents itself for you to be at the table, maybe for the first few times listen to how everyone interacts with one another. See how the different people react as ideas are given back and forth and learn a little bit about the politics of the room. I have seen all too often where someone will be invited to a meeting and make inappropriate comments, only to never be invited back again.

Prepare For The Meeting

Many times it is easy to think you know everything about a subject of the meeting and just think you can go in a wing the information. If you are presented with the opportunity to move to one of the coveted seats at the table you would like to occupy, take the time and do a little extra research on the subject. Many times I will try to set up a premeeting with the person who has invited me to go through what questions may come up in the meeting. This gives you a good opportunity to go through with them and discuss the people who are going to be there and talk about what they are going to want to hear about before you show up. Personally, I am would always like to over-prepare for a meeting, then I can feel better about the information I have to give everyone.

Follow Up

One of the best things I have always found to help solidify your seat at the table is by following up with a few of the people after the meeting. If there was a particular person who asked several questions about what I was presenting or a topic I was interested in, I will try to arrange a time to go back and meet with them about the issue. This helps them see you are paying attention and you want to be an active participant in the conversations. These conversations are the ones, especially in the beginning, where you can show your commitment to the group as well as even start to bounce some ideas off a small group of people. If your ideas make sense to the smaller group they might have merit for the larger group and you might even pick up a few supports along the way.

Follow Through On Commitments

As always, if there was something you were asked to do or bring back to the group, be sure to get them taken care and have them ready for the next meeting. Even if they do not bring them up in the next meeting this will give you a good excuse to go follow up later with people to let them know you had done the assignment you were given.

All of us want to have a seat at the table, to have our ideas heard and to be part of a larger organization. These are just four things I have observed that have helped me earn my spot at many of the seats I sit in.
