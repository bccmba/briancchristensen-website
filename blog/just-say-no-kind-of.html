Just Say No - Kind of

One of the first things I was told when I started out on my Product path was to learn to just say no to everything. I can still remember sitting in my CSPM class and hearing my instructor say that over and over to us. This makes sense, right? There is always going to be more and more work to do and there is no way you can do all of it. So it stands to reason you are going to need to learn to tell them no, you are not going to work on their project.

Learning to say no was then followed with you have to get used to disappointing people and not having too many friends. This is where I had the bigger problem. My personality type is being a people pleaser and one of my favorite sayings is “T-Shirts are like friends, you can never too many of them.” (Last check I had over 50 t-shirts in my closet). I had started class excited to learn all about my new job and new career and now I was having huge doubts if I had made the right choice. However, through the course of my career, I have come up with a couple of ways to work with this.

“Let me check on that”

This has to be my most favorite way to get around the just say no dilemma. Instead of dismissing my stakeholder's ideas and causing potential rifts, I just say let me check on that and get back to you. Like all of life’s good lessons, I learned this one the hard way.

Sitting in a meeting, one of my stakeholders presented an idea for a change to the product they felt very strongly about. I found this out a few minutes later, when I was asked if we could work on this and sticking to my guns I told them no. We were currently working on another large initiative and I felt like this was going to derail us from getting this work done. My stakeholder took my no very personally and we had a back and forth exchange after the meeting that did not end well. I was still frustrated about this when one of my developers came by later to ask me a question. Sensing there was something wrong, they asked what had happened and I laid out the whole story to them. At the end of my telling of the story, the developer said, “Well that is a silly thing to get upset about. Most of what they are asking for really is not that much different than what we are working on now we could probably just sneak it in.”

I now felt awful, and after eating a large slice of humble pie, went to go talk to this stakeholder and let them know what my developer had said. However, the stakeholder was not on the same page as I was and even though I was going to be able to get their work done for them, my initial rejection of their idea caused a tension between us that made working together very hard for a long time.

Now, instead of just jumping to no, I will throw out the idea of letting me check on it and getting back to them. This then gives me an opportunity to take a few minutes to double-check our current workload, review some of the backlog items and check with my team to see what they think. Then when I go back and have my conversation with the stakeholder we can have a great conversation. The answer might still be no but now I can show them why and what other projects are in the way.

By not just jumping to no, I can give my stakeholders the sense I do care about their project and I will see what I can do to help get it prioritized where both the stakeholder and the team feel good about the work.

Help Establish an MVP

The other key thing I will try to do instead of saying no is to see what the stakeholder is really after. I have had many times when the stakeholder has presented this very large and elaborate feature that would derail the team for a long time. However, once I start asking questions about what they trying to accomplish with their idea sometimes we can find another solution.

One stakeholder asked us to add an elaborate community platform on the website. They wanted a place where our users would be able to ask questions and other users or our staff could answer these questions. It made sense, but it was going to be a big undertaking. I then asked why they wanted to put this into the system. The response I got back was something like, “Well we get a lot of questions and it would be nice to have a place for people to ask and answer them.” After further discussion, the stakeholder said they really only get about the same 10 questions all of the time. So we floated the idea of creating a FAQ section on our product. Take these top 10 questions they have and direct people to the FAQ to get an answer. If it worked and we needed to expand we could move on from there. We never built anything more than the FAQ page because once we had it in place, it completely solved the problem the stakeholder came to use with.

Although the elaborate community platform would have been amazing for our website, it would have to take a lot of time to build. The FAQ page was simple and we were able to knock it out in one sprint. My favorite part of the whole story though is we did not just say no and move on. Instead, I gained the trust of the stakeholder who knew whatever issue they had, we could find a way to help them.

Hopefully, the next time you are tempted to say no to something one of your stakeholders brings up, you take a min and see what other options you have.
