The Product Manager Monomyth

A few years ago I left an interview with the hiring manager telling me I was not the right fit for the job. My previous experience as a Product Manager had all been in the Healthcare sector and the position I was applying for was in Fintech. As I was driving home I had a flashback to when I was rejected for the first credit card I had ever applied for. The reason I had been rejected was because I had never had a credit card before. In my head I got stuck in this loop, how am I ever going to get a credit card if one of the requirements is to already have a credit card.

On the drive, I felt like I had stumbled into another one of the loops. How could I ever get a job as a Product Manager in an industry other than Healthcare, just because I had never worked as a Product Manager in that industry before? Over the next little while, I started to do some research because there had to be someone who had moved industries as a Product Manager and I wanted to find them and see how they made the transition. It was during this time I discovered Joseph Campbell.

Joseph Campbell and the Monomyth

One evening, while looking for something to watch, I stumbled upon Myths and Monsters' show on Netflix. For me, it was a great series where the host, Nicholas Day, goes through a variety of stories and legends and talks about where they come from and why they have persisted in our culture today. Some of these topics include: "Heros and Villans", "The Wild Unknown" and "Love and Betrayal" to name a few. However the topic I was most fascinated with that of Joseph Campbell and his idea of the Monomyth.

In the 1940s, Joseph Campbell began a study of mythological legends when he noticed they all contained the same pattern. As he began to start using this pattern, he was able to apply this pattern to more and more legends until he was able to develop the monomyth. In his book, The Hero With A Thousand Faces, Campbell laid out the 17 steps he was able to identify from his study. Although not all monomyths contain all stages, but instead my focus only on one stage, while others may deal with all of the stages in a different order. The 17 stages are often organized into three acts and broken out as follows:

  1. Departure - where the hero lives in the ordinary world and receives a call to go on an adventure
    • The Call to Adventure
    • Refusal of the Call
    • Supernatural Aid
    • The Crossing of the First Threshold
    • Belly of the Whale
  2. Initiation - where the hero to an unknown or "special world" and faces tasks and trials, either alone or with assistance
    • The Road of Trials
    • The Meeting with the Goddess
    • Woman as the Temptress
    • Atonement with the Father
    • Apotheosis
    • The Ultimate Boon
  3. Return - where the hero returns to the ordinary world with his rewards
    • Refusal of the Return
    • The Magic Flight
    • Rescue for Without
    • The crossing of the Return Threshold
    • Master of the Two Worlds
    • Freedom to Live

Throughout the years, there have been many people who have read and have been fascinated with the monomyth and have used it in helping with their own storytelling. One of the most famous is George Lucas, who attributed Campbells work as one of the influences of his creation of the Star Wars books and movies.

Monomyth and the Product Manager

As I watched this documentary and listened to the description of the monomyth, I could not help but think back to the interview. In my mind, I saw no difference between the heroes in Campbell's stories and the Product Managers I knew. I could see how a Product Manager could start out on a similar path as a hero, where we are taking our product on a journey. We start by making a departure from the status quo and go on our own Call to Adventure.

Although all 17 steps Cambell laid out may not apply to the process of developing a road map and building out our products, I feel there are a lot a similarities that I want to discuss in future articles. For now I want to finish where I started.

Although at the time I did not have any Fintech experience, I did have several years experience as a Product Manager. I had been through multiple monomyth cycles, even going on multiple journeys with different products at the same time. It is this experience that I feel should matter the most.

Recently I had the opportunity to have a reversal of roles and I now I was the hiring manager. As I began to look through resumes it would have been easy to eliminate people because they did not have the exact experience I was looking for. However, I took a step back and looked at the experiences my candidates had. I looked at the people who had succeffully showed they were understood product developement and how to get it from point A to point B just because their were the Product Manager.

As I interviewed applicates, I asked them questions about their process for product development. We discussed different tactics they had learned and used. We talked about the strengths and weaknesses of these different tactics so I could get a better understanding of what they have done in the past and how successful they have been. I was looking for the Product Manager who had been on the hero journey and wanted to see what rewards they had returned with.

In the end, I ended up hiring someone who did not have the exact skill set for the product they were going to managing. I ended up hiring the person who had been on a number of journeys, had come how with different rewards and I knew would be able to do the same with the roll we were going to ask them to fill.
