My story

I started in account management.
Frustration drove me to engineering.

My career didn't start in product or engineering — it started on the client side. As an account manager, my job was to understand what clients needed and make sure they got it. I was good at it. I understood stakeholders, I understood relationships, and I understood what it meant to be accountable to someone else's success.

But there was a constant friction I couldn't shake. I was always waiting — waiting on development for data, waiting for system updates, waiting for answers to questions my clients needed answered now. I could see exactly what my clients needed. I just couldn't get there fast enough.

I knew there had to be a better way. So I learned engineering. Not to become a developer — but to stop being dependent on one. I started in reporting and analytics, picking up SQL, learning how data actually flowed through systems. And that changed everything.

Suddenly I could pull my own data instead of waiting on reports. I could sit with engineers and have real conversations, not just translate requirements. I could understand why something was hard to build, not just that it was. That foundation — built out of client-side frustration — made me a fundamentally different kind of product leader.

20 years of learning
by doing hard things.

One of the most formative projects of my career came when I was asked to lead a complex distributor compensation plan overhaul — a system I didn't fully understand at the time. Commission structures, rank qualifications, payout rules across multiple markets. It felt overwhelming.

Instead of stepping back, I dove in. I asked every question. I sat with stakeholders from finance, compliance, and sales operations until I could see how the whole system fit together. That project became one of my most valuable learning experiences — and the foundation for the compensation work I still lead today.

That's been the pattern. You won't always feel ready. Do it anyway. That's where the growth is.

How I think about product
01
Strategy is a conversation, not a document

The most successful product strategies I've seen aren't PDFs — they're living conversations between customer data, business goals, and evolving capabilities. Strategy should be a shared language, not a top-down directive. Start every session by identifying what won't change. Adapt the rest.

02
Execution beats elegance, every time

A perfect roadmap means nothing without consistent delivery. I favor repeatable execution over complex ambition — lean processes, shared visibility, and transparency over perfection. The goal is to become more predictable, which builds trust at every level. Kill hero culture. Elevate frameworks and feedback loops.

03
Great PMs empower — they don't control

My job isn't to be the smartest person in the room. It's to unlock the team's brilliance. Provide the "why" and let teams define the "how." When people feel trusted to own their work, they deliver outcomes no single person could have planned for. Culture is the product too.
