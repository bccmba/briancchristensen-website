The problem

Most distributors made one
purchase. Then nothing.

In a direct sales model, the relationship between a sponsor and their new enrollee determines everything. A new distributor who makes one purchase and never hears from their sponsor again will almost certainly churn. There's no product failure, no bad experience — just the absence of any reason to come back.

The data confirmed what intuition already suggested: distributors who made it to a 2nd or 3rd purchase were significantly more likely to stay. If you could move someone past the first drop-off point, the long-term retention picture changed dramatically. The challenge was making that happen systematically — not just for the motivated sponsors who would have done it anyway, but across 20+ global markets simultaneously.

The incentive and the desired
behaviour weren't aligned.

The existing compensation structure rewarded recruitment — bringing new distributors in. It did not reward development — keeping them engaged through those critical early cycles. Sponsors had no financial reason to follow up with a new enrollee a week after sign-up, call before the second order cycle, or actively coach someone toward their next purchase.

You can train people and encourage the right behaviour, but if the compensation plan doesn't reward it, the behaviour won't happen at scale. This wasn't a training problem or a communication problem. It was a structural incentive problem — and the fix had to be structural too.

The solution

Make sponsor engagement
financially worth doing.

The core of the redesign was the Milestone Bonus — a reward paid to sponsors every time one of their new enrollees made a purchase within the first 6 months of joining. Not a one-time sign-up bonus. A recurring payment tied directly to the behaviour we needed: ongoing sponsor engagement that kept new enrollees active through the critical early cycles.

The structure was deliberate. The more a sponsor worked with their new enrollees — encouraging them, supporting them, helping them make that second and third purchase — the more the sponsor earned. For the first time, the financial incentive and the desired behaviour pointed the same direction.

Brian's role spanned the full initiative. He worked as a business stakeholder during the plan design phase, helping shape the incentive structure and the logic behind the 6-month window. Then — as the product lead — he owned the full build and global rollout: requirements definition, cross-functional alignment across compensation, finance, engineering, compliance, and global market operations teams, and zero-disruption delivery across 20+ markets on July 11, 2025. That dual perspective on both the business rationale and the technical delivery was critical in keeping a complex, multi-stakeholder effort on track.

Compounding effect
What I learned

Behaviour follows incentives,
not intentions.

You can train distributors to support their new enrollees. You can show them the data, run the workshops, share the best practices. But if the compensation plan doesn't reward that behaviour financially, it won't happen consistently across 20+ global markets simultaneously.

The lesson isn't specific to direct sales. If the behaviour you want isn't happening, look at what the incentive structure actually rewards — not what you intend it to reward. The two are often different. The fix here wasn't training or communication. It was structure.

Real improvement shows up in
34 weeks of data, not one.

The retention number moved immediately in the week of launch. That's a good signal — it means distributors noticed and responded. But a first-week lift doesn't prove structural change. It proves awareness.

The more important question was whether the behaviour held as the launch novelty faded. It did. The most recent data is the strongest in the entire post-launch period. That's what a structural improvement looks like — not a spike, but a new floor that keeps rising.
