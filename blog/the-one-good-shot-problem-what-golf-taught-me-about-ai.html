The One Good Shot Problem:
What Golf Taught Me About AI

Why AI success feels like fireworks — and why it actually looks like a lighthouse.

Tomorrow morning I'm teeing it up with my boys. I'm an average weekend warrior — no illusions about my handicap — but I'll be out there grinding through 18 holes like I always do. Statistically, at least 15 will be unremarkable. A few will be genuinely ugly. But there will be one shot. One perfect, pure, inexplicable shot that flies exactly where I meant it to.

That one shot is why I keep coming back.

I work in AI professionally, and this week I came across an illustration by Clare Kitching that stopped me mid-scroll. The top half showed fireworks with the caption: "What AI success feels like." The bottom half showed a lighthouse — steady, purposeful, weathering a storm — labelled: "What AI success actually requires."

I immediately thought of golf.

The Fireworks Trap

In golf, the fireworks are that one perfect drive, the long putt that somehow drops, the chip-in from off the green that makes your playing partners shake their heads in disbelief. It feels like genius. It feels like you've cracked the code. And for about thirty seconds, you genuinely believe you might be better at this game than you thought.

AI has the same problem. A slick demo. A jaw-dropping output. A moment where the technology does something that feels like magic and everyone in the room leans forward. Leadership gets excited. Budget gets approved. Expectations quietly — dangerously — inflate.

The fireworks are real. But they are not the whole story. Not even close.

What the Lighthouse Actually Represents

Clare's illustration annotates the lighthouse with six things AI success actually requires. As someone who spends their working life in this space — and their weekends on a golf course — I can tell you that each one maps perfectly to what separates a good round from a great one.

Consistent Performance
The lighthouse doesn't shine once. In golf, consistency beats brilliance over 18 holes. A model that performs reliably at 85% across thousands of real-world queries is worth infinitely more than one that nails 100% in a demo and falls apart in production.
A Clear Purpose
The lighthouse beam doesn't wander. The best golfers know their game — which club to pull, which risk is worth taking, which is just ego. AI projects that succeed are solving a defined problem for a defined user. Not trying to do everything at once.
Known Limits
This is the one most people skip — in golf and in AI. I know I'm not making that 220-yard carry over water. Playing within your limits isn't weakness; it's strategy. The AI projects that blow up are the ones where nobody drew a boundary around what the system should and shouldn't attempt.
Human Oversight
That figure standing in the lighthouse door? That's the keeper. In golf, it's the partner who tells you the pin is further back than it looks. No AI system — none — should be running without a human in the loop when the stakes are high. The keeper isn't optional.
Ongoing Effort (Spare Bulbs)
The lighthouse doesn't maintain itself. Range sessions, putting practice, video review — unglamorous, nobody posts it on Instagram, but it's what moves the needle. The model you deploy on day one is not the model that will serve you on day 365. Maintenance, retraining, monitoring — that's the spare bulbs.
Managing Edge Cases
The storm clouds in the illustration aren't decorative. The real world throws things at you that the practice round never prepared you for — a bad lie in the rough, a sudden wind shift. AI faces the same: the weird input, the edge case the training data never covered. The lighthouse stands through the storm.

The Shot That Brings You Back

Here's the thing I've realised: that one perfect shot isn't a lie. It's not false advertising. It's a genuine glimpse of what's possible when everything comes together — the right technique, the right conditions, the right moment of focus.

The AI demo that takes everyone's breath away isn't a lie either. The technology really can do that. The question is whether you've built the lighthouse around it.

Tomorrow, I'll chunk a few shots into the rough. I'll three-putt at least once. I'll take a drop I'm not proud of. And then somewhere around the 14th hole, I'll stripe one right down the middle and walk off the tee feeling like a completely different golfer.

The trick — in golf and in AI — is to stop chasing only the fireworks, and start building the lighthouse that makes them possible more often.
