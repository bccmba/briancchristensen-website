The Bazinsky Principle: A Lesson in Prioritization and Collaboration

Throughout my career, I’ve often reflected on pivotal moments that shaped my approach to leadership, collaboration, and prioritization. One of the most memorable came when I was a brand-new Product Manager, navigating a challenging yet insightful situation involving competing priorities across the company.

It all began with a series of meetings. Several divisions were adamant that their project should be my top priority. Each team insisted their initiative was critical, claiming it was the number one priority of our COO, Jordan Bazinsky. A phrase I heard repeatedly was, “This is Jordan’s top priority.”

Some team members grew visibly frustrated when I explained that I needed to assess everyone’s priorities before making a decision. One person even asked, “What’s the point of talking to them? My project is the most important—it’s Jordan’s top priority!”

The problem was clear: there could only be one “number one” project. But how was I going to solve it?

As a new Product Manager, I found myself wrestling with two strong emotions. The first was concern—how was I going to satisfy all these stakeholders? The second was nervousness. I realized the only way to resolve the issue was to step far outside my comfort zone and reach out directly to Jordan.

Normally, I’m comfortable talking to people, but for some reason, calling the COO of my company felt intimidating. Perhaps it was because my company had recently been acquired by a Fortune 500 corporation, and I was stepping into uncharted territory, interacting with someone I’d never met in an organization far larger than I was used to. I was also hesitant because, as a new Product Manager, I wanted to demonstrate that I could handle everything on my own. Calling the COO felt like admitting defeat.

But I knew there was no other way. So, I swallowed my pride, pushed past the fear of looking incompetent—or even being fired—and picked up the phone.

To my surprise, Jordan was not only approachable but also incredibly supportive. He took the time to listen, thoughtfully discuss the competing requests, and clarify the broader company goals. Together, we determined what truly deserved to be prioritized.

That conversation turned out to be a defining moment in my career. Jordan’s encouragement and guidance helped me feel more confident in my role as a Product Manager. More importantly, it taught me some invaluable lessons:

  1. True Prioritization Requires Collaboration
    When multiple stakeholders claim priority, the best way forward is to engage directly with them. Open, honest conversations foster alignment and prevent wasted effort.
  2. Stakeholder Influence Must Be Balanced With Organizational Goals
    It’s easy for individual teams to focus solely on their objectives. As leaders, we must take a step back and assess what’s best for the organization as a whole.

Over the years, I’ve shared this story countless times with teams and individuals I’ve managed. It’s become a framework for navigating competing priorities, and I now call it the Bazinsky Principle: a straightforward yet powerful way to resolve conflicts, identify true priorities, and foster collaboration.

What makes this principle so impactful isn’t just the strategy itself—it’s the mindset it encourages. Prioritization isn’t about simply saying “yes” or “no.” It’s about facilitating a dialogue that identifies what truly matters.

Reflecting on that single phone call, I’m reminded of the power of clarity and consensus. For me, it created a lasting approach to handling complex scenarios.

The next time you’re grappling with competing priorities, consider the Bazinsky Principle. Pick up the phone, have the conversation, and focus on what’s not just urgent, but truly important.
